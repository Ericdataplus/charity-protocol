🔐 The Trust Model

Trust-minimized, radically transparent execution.

We believe in honest architecture. Here is what you MUST trust:

  • ⚠️ The Executor (until confirmation-gated reimbursement)
  • ⚠️ The Charity's honesty (mitigated by photo proof & audits)
  • ⚠️ USDC stability
  • ⚠️ Third-party auditors

Here is what you DO NOT have to trust:

  • ✅ Founder's promises (On-chain v2 utilizes a 3-of-5 multisig with 48-hour timelock)
  • ✅ Hidden books (The ledger is entirely public)

🗳️ Governance Rules

Governance Parameters:

  • 7-day voting window with a 10% quorum.
  • Simple majority rule (>50%).
  • 48-hour challenge period before execution.
  • Anti-snipe extension: If the outcome flips in the final hours, voting extends.
  • Per-proposal spending caps tied to charity trust tiers.
  • Conflicts of interest must be disclosed.
  • Voting rights are revocable ONLY by a 75% supermajority governance vote for documented fraud.
  • Dead-man wind-down: If dormant for 18 months, fallback charities receive remaining funds.

⚠️ What Could Go Wrong

Complete transparency means telling you the risks too:

Smart Contract Bug

Code vulnerability could be exploited. We mitigate with audits and bug bounties.

Vendor Collusion

What if Amazon's payment address changes? Requires 67% vote + 7-day delay to update.

Charity Resells Products

A charity could sell donated goods for cash. Community can vote to remove bad actors.

Stablecoin Depeg

USDC could lose its $1 peg. We use the most regulated, audited stablecoin available.

Voting Manipulation

Coordinated groups could sway votes. Quadratic voting limits whale power.

Regulatory Shutdown

Governments could ban crypto. We comply with laws and maintain transparency.

🗺️ Roadmap

Our path from concept to decentralized charity protocol.

Stage 0: Concept Demo (Current)

  • Interactive frontend with simulated data.
  • Gathering community interest and technical feedback.

Stage 1: Entity Formation

  • Establishing a Fiscal Sponsorship or an independent 501(c)(3).
  • Handling money for charity requires strict AML/KYC compliance. We must have a legal entity to prevent the protocol from being classified as an unlicensed money transmitter.
  • Donations will only be tax-deductible once this entity is established.

Stage 2: Closed Pilot

  • Testing the Confirmation-Gated Executor model with 2-3 local charities.
  • Manual verification and execution.

Stage 3: Public Crypto Rollout

  • Opening the treasury to public USDC contributions.
  • Enforcing Quadratic Voting via decentralized identity.

Stage 4: On-Chain v2 (DAO)

  • Transitioning treasury control to a 3-of-5 multisig.
  • Smart contracts govern the Executor reimbursement flow.

❓ FAQ & Risks

Honest answers to the hardest questions.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are contributions tax-deductible?

Not yet. In Stage 0 and 1, we do not hold a 501(c)(3) status or fiscal sponsor. Once we secure that legal standing, contributions will become tax-deductible.


Why not be fully trustless?

Because physical delivery of goods requires human confirmation. Smart contracts can't look inside an Amazon box. Instead, we use a Confirmation-Gated Executor model, which minimizes trust but doesn't pretend to eliminate it.


What if the Executor runs away with the money?

The Executor uses their own funds (or a corporate account) to buy the goods. They are only reimbursed by the treasury AFTER the charity confirms receipt. If they act maliciously, they simply aren't reimbursed. The treasury is never at risk.
